Background

LANDFIRE’s Biophysical Settings(BpS) tell many stories-where ecosystems were likely to occur historically, how they looked and functioned. Officially BpSs are:

“A modeled representation of the vegetation system that may have been dominant on the landscape prior to Euro-American settlement and is based on both the current biophysical environment and an approximation of modeled past disturbance regimes. LF uses BPS to depict reference conditions of vegetation across landscapes.”

In these reports we do quick summaries of the BpSs and their associated historical fire regime attributes for each National Park to:

  1. Identify the top Biophysical Settings and their extent
  2. Calculate average annual acres burned historically
  3. Explore which fire types were most prevalent (i.e., surface, mixed or replacement)

Summary

From a quick assessment of the BpSs and their historical Mean Fire Return Intervals for Acadia National Park we found:

  • There were 21 Biophysical Settings (by name, there may be more if counting by variants). The Acadian Low-Elevation Spruce-Fir-Hardwood Forest was the most prevalent overall, with the Northern Atlantic Coastal Plain Dune and Swale having the most fire annually.
  • For the 10 most common BpSs, roughly 165 acres burned annually or 0% of Acadia National Park.
  • For the 10 most common BpSs, Mean Fire Return Intervals for all fire types combined ranged from 4 to 1127 years, with an area weighted mean of 734 (weighted by acres of BpSs)

Fire Return Interval and Severity by Biophysical Setting

The BpS dataset includes multiple attributes including:

  • BPS_CODE Biophysical Settings (BpS) code used by LANDFIRE
  • ZONE National Land Cover Database Map Zone the BpS relates to
  • BPS_MODEL Unique BpS model code
  • BPS_NAME BpS Name
  • FRI_REPLAC Average historic replacement fire frequency (greater than 75% top kill of vegetation)
  • FRI_MIXED Average historic mixed fire frequency
  • FRI_SURFAC Average historic surface fire frequency
  • FRI_ALL Quantifies the average period between fires (all types).
